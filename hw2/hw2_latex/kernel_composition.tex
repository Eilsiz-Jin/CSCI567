Prove that if $k_1, k_2: \R^d \times \R^d \rightarrow \R$ are both kernel functions, then $k(\x, \x') = k_1(\x, \x')k_2(\x,\x')$ is a kernel function too.
Specifically, suppose that $\boldsymbol{\phi}_1$ and $\boldsymbol{\phi}_2$ are the corresponding mappings for $k_1$ and $k_2$ respectively.
Construct the mapping $\boldsymbol{\phi}$ that certifies $k$ being a kernel function.
